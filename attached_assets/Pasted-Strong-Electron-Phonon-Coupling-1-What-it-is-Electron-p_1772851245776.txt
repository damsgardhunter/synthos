What it is

Electron-phonon coupling describes how strongly lattice vibrations interact with electrons.

When atoms vibrate, they distort the lattice potential.
Electrons feel this distortion and attract each other indirectly, forming Cooper pairs.

2. High Phonon Frequencies (ω_log)
What it is

Higher lattice vibration frequencies increase Tc directly.

In BCS/Eliashberg:

Tc ∝ phonon frequency

Light atoms vibrate faster.

Where it appears
Material	High-frequency atom
LaH10	hydrogen
H3S	hydrogen
MgB2	boron

Hydrogen produces phonon modes above 1000 cm⁻¹.

Why hydrides dominate

Hydrogen:

mass = 1

Phonon frequency roughly scales as:

ω ~ sqrt(k / M)

Lower mass → higher ω.

3. High Density of States at the Fermi Level (DOS(EF))
What it is

DOS(EF) = number of electronic states available at the Fermi energy.

More states = more electrons available to pair.

Where it appears

Examples:

Material	DOS(EF) feature
MgB2	σ-band peak
LaH10	hydrogen bands
YBCO	van Hove singularity
Important effect

Many high-Tc materials sit near a van Hove singularity.

This causes:

DOS(EF) spike

which amplifies pairing.

Implementation targets

Your system should reward:

DOS(EF) / avgDOS > 2

You already implemented something similar with flat-band detection.

4. Fermi Surface Nesting
What it is

Nesting occurs when large portions of the Fermi surface match when shifted by a wave vector.

This enhances interactions like:

phonons
spin fluctuations
charge density waves
Where it appears

Examples:

Material	Nesting feature
FeSe	electron–hole pocket nesting
BaFe2As2	Γ–M nesting
MgB2	σ-band nesting
Implementation targets

Good nesting occurs when:

quasi-2D band structure
layered structures
multiple Fermi sheets

This is why layered materials dominate high-Tc systems.

Certain crystal structures appear repeatedly in high-Tc systems.

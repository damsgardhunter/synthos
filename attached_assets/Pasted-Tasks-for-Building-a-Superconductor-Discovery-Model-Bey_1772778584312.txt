Tasks for Building a Superconductor Discovery Model Beyond Current Physics Limits
1. Separate Physical Constraints from Physical Assumptions

Definition:
Physical constraints are laws that cannot be violated (e.g., charge conservation). Physical assumptions are empirical trends from existing materials that may not hold universally.

Task:

Hard-code fundamental constraints:

charge neutrality

thermodynamic stability bounds

positive phonon frequencies (no imaginary modes)

Fermi statistics

Do not hard-code empirical limits such as:

Tc ceilings

maximum electron-phonon coupling values

hydrogen-only high-Tc rules

Goal: Allow the model to explore outside human expectations without producing physically impossible materials.

2. Use Physics-Rich Descriptors Instead of Composition Only

Definition:
Descriptors are numerical features describing a material’s physical properties. Composition alone lacks information about electronic behavior.

Task:
Use descriptors including:

Density of States at the Fermi Level (DOS(EF))
Number of electronic states available for conduction.

Orbital Character at EF
Whether electrons come from s, p, or d orbitals.

Phonon Density of States
Distribution of lattice vibration frequencies.

Bond Stiffness / Force Constants
Measure of lattice rigidity and phonon strength.

Charge Transfer Metrics
Electron movement between atoms.

Structural Topology Graphs
Representation of crystal connectivity.

Goal: Predict pairing mechanisms more accurately than composition-based models.

3. Switch from Property Prediction to Inverse Design

Definition:
Inverse design searches for structures that maximize a desired physical phenomenon instead of predicting properties of known materials.

Task:
Optimize candidate materials for:

Pairing susceptibility – tendency of electrons to form Cooper pairs

Electron–boson coupling strength

Fermi surface nesting

Low-energy collective modes

Goal: Directly search for materials likely to superconduct rather than predicting Tc after the fact.

4. Evaluate Multiple Pairing Mechanisms

Definition:
Superconductivity can arise from several mechanisms, not just phonon-mediated pairing.

Task:
Include evaluation models for:

Electron-phonon coupling – conventional BCS superconductivity

Spin fluctuations – magnetic interaction pairing

Excitonic pairing – electron–hole coupling

Plasmonic pairing – charge oscillation mediated pairing

Flat-band superconductivity – pairing enhanced by nearly zero band dispersion

Goal: Discover superconductors outside conventional BCS mechanisms.

5. Implement Generative Crystal Structure Discovery

Definition:
Generative models create entirely new crystal structures instead of screening known ones.

Task:
Use generative approaches such as:

diffusion models for crystal graphs

variational autoencoders

evolutionary crystal structure search

Generate structures featuring:

unusual bonding networks

cluster-based lattices

mixed dimensional systems

unconventional coordination environments

Goal: Discover materials not present in existing databases.

6. Target Instability Boundaries

Definition:
The strongest superconductivity often occurs near competing phases or lattice instabilities.

Task:
Search for materials with:

soft phonon modes – low-energy lattice vibrations

charge density wave proximity

magnetic quantum critical points

metal–insulator transition boundaries

Goal: Locate materials near phase transitions where pairing interactions become strong.

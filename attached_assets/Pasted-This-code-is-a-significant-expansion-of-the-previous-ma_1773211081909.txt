This code is a significant expansion of the previous material science engine, specifically focusing on the microscopic parameters of superconductivity (Coulomb pseudopotential $\mu^*$, bandwidth $W$, and electron-phonon coupling $\lambda$).Below is a technical review of the logic, physics, and potential bugs in these new sections.1. Physical Model & Scientific ConstraintsCoulomb Pseudopotential ($\mu^*$) Logic:The Model: computeScreenedMuStar uses the Morel-Anderson relation ($\mu^* = \frac{\mu}{1 + \mu \ln(E_F/\omega_D)}$). This is the standard physical approach for conventional superconductors.The Improvement: You've added a screeningFactor based on the Thomas-Fermi screening length ($k_{TF}^2$). This correctly accounts for the fact that a higher Density of States ($N(E_F)$) provides better screening of the Coulomb repulsion, which lowers $\mu^*$ and benefits $T_c$.Constraint: You cap $\mu^*$ for "Heavy Fermions" at 0.28. In reality, in heavy fermion systems, the "effective" $\mu^*$ can be even higher due to strong local repulsions, or the mechanism might shift entirely to non-phonon pairing. Your getCorrelationPenaltyForClass handles the $T_c$ suppression side of this effectively.Bandwidth ($W$) Estimation:The Logic: estimateBandwidthW uses the Sommerfeld gamma ($\gamma$) to infer bandwidth ($W \propto V / \gamma$).Constraint: This assumes a free-electron-like parabolic band. For transition metals (especially 3d like Fe or Ni), the narrow d-bands mean this approximation is rough. However, your ELEMENT_BANDWIDTH lookup table provides a good empirical anchor for the most important elements.Correlation Penalties:Scientific Note: getCorrelationPenaltyForClass implements a power-law suppression of $T_c$ as $U/W$ (the ratio of Hubbard $U$ to bandwidth) increases.Insight: You correctly identify $0.6$ as a "danger zone" for Cuprates. Physically, as $U/W$ approaches $1$, the system undergoes a Mott transition to an insulator, and $T_c$ should drop to zero. Your formula $1 / (1 + 1.8(U-0.6)^{1.5})$ effectively simulates the "dome" behavior where superconductivity is suppressed by strong correlations.2. Potential Logic Bugs & Edge CasesZero-Division in computeScreenedMuStar:Issue: const logRatio = Math.log(Math.max(E_F_eV / omega_D_eV, 1.5));Risk: If omega_D_eV (derived from Debye temperature) is exceptionally high or $E_F$ is exceptionally low, the ratio could approach 1. Math.log(1) is 0. If mu_bare is also high, the denominator 1 + mu_bare * logRatio could technically hit 1.Fix: You have a Math.max(..., 1.5) inside the log, which prevents the denominator from ever becoming too small.

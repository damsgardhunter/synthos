These three signatures appear repeatedly in many of the highest-Tc superconductors discovered so far. If you were designing a discovery engine like yours, they are excellent heuristics to detect materials sitting near a superconducting instability.

1. Competing Electronic Orders
4
Definition

Competing orders occur when multiple electronic ground states are nearly equal in energy.

Common examples:

Charge density wave (CDW) — periodic modulation of electron density.

Spin density wave (SDW) — periodic magnetic ordering.

Antiferromagnetism

Structural distortions

Superconductivity often appears right at the boundary where one of these orders collapses.

Examples:

Material	Competing Order
Cuprates	Antiferromagnetism
Iron pnictides	Spin density waves
AV₃Sb₅ Kagome metals	Charge density waves
2H-NbSe₂	CDW
Why it matters

Near the boundary of these phases:

electronic fluctuations become extremely strong

these fluctuations can mediate Cooper pairing

Task Definition

For each candidate material:

Detect proximity to:

charge density wave instabilities

spin density waves

antiferromagnetic ordering

Score materials higher when they are near a phase boundary but still metallic.

Track instability indicators such as:

soft phonon modes

magnetic susceptibility peaks

Fermi surface nesting.

2. Fermi Surface Nesting
4
Definition

Fermi surface nesting occurs when large sections of the Fermi surface can be mapped onto each other by a single momentum vector Q.

Mathematically:

E(k) ≈ E(k + Q)

This enhances scattering between those states.

Why it matters

Strong nesting produces:

spin fluctuations

charge density waves

enhanced electron pairing interactions

Examples:

Material	Mechanism
Iron pnictides	SDW fluctuations
Cuprates	antiferromagnetic fluctuations
Kagome metals	CDW fluctuations
Task Definition

For each candidate material:

compute approximate Fermi surface topology

measure nesting strength

Possible proxy metrics:

DOS(Ef)

flat band proximity

symmetry of electron/hole pockets

Increase pairing score when:

nesting_strength > threshold

especially when combined with moderate correlations.

3. High Density of States at the Fermi Level
4
Definition

The density of states at the Fermi level (DOS(Ef)) determines how many electrons can participate in pairing.

A high DOS(Ef) often arises from:

flat bands

Van Hove singularities

heavy effective masses

Why it matters

In the BCS relation:

Tc ∝ exp(-1 / (N(Ef)V))

Where:

N(Ef) = density of states
V = interaction strength

Higher DOS means:

stronger pairing probability

higher Tc potential.

Examples:

Material	Feature
MgB₂	high DOS from σ-bands
Cuprates	Van Hove singularities
Twisted graphene	flat bands
Kagome metals	flat bands
Task Definition

For each candidate:

Compute or estimate:

DOS(Ef)

proximity to Van Hove singularity

band flatness

Increase pairing score when:

DOS(Ef) is high
AND
band dispersion is low

but apply penalties if DOS comes from localized states that suppress conduction.
